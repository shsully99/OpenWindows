About Open Windows

This is a free to use web application whose purpose is to assist in the specification of glazing and ventilation for newly built and converted residences in England and Wales, in places where noise intrusion is considered to be an issue.


It is intended principally for design teams involved in the specification of glazing and ventilation as well as other practicioners, such as Environmental Health Officers who wish to ascertain that the design adequately protect residents from excessive noise internally.


Based on the levels of noise outside a dwelling, which have been determined generally by measurement surveys, predictive modelling (or a combination of both), the application allows the user to select a specification of glazing which will ensure that the residents are not exposed to excessive noise levels inside a dwelling. In doing so, it allows the design team to take into account the effect of other elements of the building facade, notably the ventilation.


The web application was created by Sulltech Ltd.

Overview

When a new dwelling is formed it is necessary to design the building envelope so that residents are not exposed to excessive noise intrusion from external noise sources which in the UK is most commonly road traffic. Since the windows are the element of a room's facade offering the least resistance to noise ingress, windows must be specified to ensure that that they resist noise adequately. Generally the two factors affecting a window's performance are the thickness of its glazing (thicker the better) and in the case of secondary or double glazing the distance of the void between the panes (the greater the better). Residents however, will generally prefer the option of opening windows to ventilate their rooms (but also for for other reasons). Once a window is open sufficiently for any of these reasons, it is the opening in the window that becomes the most significant source of noise ingress. So when specifying the glazing one should aim to achieve satisfactory levels for residents with windows at least partially open . If satisfactory internal levels cannot be achieved by such means then the "next best" is to try and achieve this with trickle and wall vents, though many of them are likely to be necessary for sufficient background ventilation. If noise levels excessive with trickle vents then designers must look to other strategies such as facade redesign, re-orientation of rooms away from noise sources or, as generally takes place in the UK, use of a mechanical ventilation system to provide background ventilation to residents


With glazing and ventilation decided one must still consider the effects of overheating on residents. The M+E team may assume that overheating be controlled by the opening of windows. However, if a room is subjected to high noise levels then opening a window to control overheating will result in the residents being exposed to excessive noise levels. The effect will be greater on south-facing facade (where solar gain is greater) than on a north-facing one, greater on a facade facing a noise source than one shielded from a noise source and greater in south of England and London that the north due to temperature differences. Again, as with ventilation, physical design measures may help (reduced glazing areas, windows shutter) and again a last resort a mechanical solution may be reqiored. These issues have been contended with by desgin teams for many years, often unsatisfactily and it is accepted that in many new dwellings occupants may have to make an undesirable choice between acoustic quality or indoor air quality and thermal comfort.


In January 2020, the IOA and ANC jointly published the Acoustics Ventilation and Overheating Design Guide(AVOG). The aim of this was to provide an integrated approach to the design of new dwellings with respect to noise intrusion from external sources, the need for ventilation and the need to avoid overheating. The document proposes that in the first instance designers should specify glazing and ventilation to ensure adequate noise levels internally. The two "metrics" it refers to are the 16 hour daytime average noise level (LAeq16hours) and the 8 hour night-time average noise level (LAeq8hours).

For daytime a design target is given of LAeq16h 35 dBA in living rooms and bedrooms and Laeq 16h 40 dBA in dining rooms.

For night-time bedrooms a design target is given of LAeq,8gh 30 dBA and in addition the 10th highest maximum noise level Lamax should not exceed LAmax 45 dB to avoid sleep distrubance.

These levels are generally determined by measurement survey at the location of the survey or estimated by predictive modelling methods.

For the consideration of overheating a 2 stage assessment is recommended, average noise levels are relaxed for the overheating condition inside a dwelling, given that it is deemed to occur less frequently. An additonal measure for night-time maximum noise level that may be typically achieved. A single noise event above LAmax 65 dBA within a bedroom may cause an awwakening. So, taking the above into consideration, a design team specifying glazing and determining a ventilation/overheating strategy for a bedroom will need to know what the noise level will be inside a room for their design for all 4 of those noise criteria. And of course they may additionally need to know what those criteria will be with the windows open, which is the "ideal" state to achieve (other factors permtting).

This web app allows user to calcualte the effect on each of these internal levels for a particular facade. In addition it permits these to be shared with other members of the design team.
